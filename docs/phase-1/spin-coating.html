Spin Coating Protocol

Depositing uniform thin films of egg white resist onto glass substrates using the 3D printed spin coater.

Materials

  • Prepared egg white resist (see preparation)
  • Glass microscope slides
  • Isopropyl alcohol 99%
  • Calibrated spin coater
  • Plastic pipettes (3mL graduated preferred)
  • Lint-free wipes or clean paper towels

Procedure

1. Clean the substrate

Wipe the glass slide with isopropyl alcohol using a lint-free wipe. Allow to air dry completely (~30 seconds). A clean, grease-free surface is essential for uniform wetting and coating adhesion.

Test for cleanliness: A clean glass slide should show uniform water sheeting when breathed on. If water beads up, the surface has residual grease — re-clean.

2. Mount on spin coater

Place the slide on the spin coater chuck. Secure with a small dot of double-sided tape at the center, or use vacuum if your coater supports it. Verify the slide is centered and level by spinning briefly at low speed and checking for wobble.

3. Dispense resist

Using a plastic pipette, deposit approximately 300μL (~6 drops) of egg white resist onto the center of the slide. Dispense slowly to avoid bubbles. Do not touch the pipette tip to the slide surface.

4. Spin

Start the spin coater at your target RPM. Hold for 30 seconds. The resist will spread outward and excess will be flung off, leaving a uniform thin film.

Thickness vs. Speed

From Jiang et al. 2017 (Figure S11), using undiluted filtered albumen:

Spin SpeedFilm ThicknessUse Case
1000 RPM~900 nmThick — easy to see, most forgiving for initial experiments
2000 RPM~450 nmGate dielectric range for ZnO TFT (Phase 2)
3000 RPM~300 nmStandard photoresist thickness
4000 RPM~200 nmThin resist for high resolution
6000 RPM~100 nmMinimum achievable thickness

For thinner films, dilute the albumen with distilled water before coating. A 1:1 dilution should approximately halve the thickness at the same spin speed. This has not been characterized in the literature — it's an area for experimentation.

Initial Thickness Mapping Experiment

Before running exposure experiments, coat 5 slides at different speeds to understand your specific setup:

  1. Coat slide A at 1000 RPM (~900nm)
  2. Coat slide B at 2000 RPM (~450nm)
  3. Coat slide C at 3000 RPM (~300nm)
  4. Coat slide D at 4000 RPM (~200nm)
  5. Coat slide E at 6000 RPM (~100nm)

After drying, tilt each slide under a light source. Thin-film interference colors (rainbow tint) indicate the film is thin and somewhat uniform. Consistent color across the surface = good uniformity. Splotchy or streaked = spin coater balance issue or contaminated surface.

5. Dry

Let the coated slide air dry at room temperature for 5 minutes, or place on a hot plate at 40°C for 2 minutes. Do not exceed 60°C at this stage — excessive heat will prematurely denature the protein and reduce photosensitivity.

Troubleshooting

ProblemCauseFix
Film is streaky or non-uniformSpin coater imbalance or off-center dispensingRe-balance chuck, center substrate, dispense at exact center
Film has comets/radial streaksParticles in the resist or on the substrateRe-filter resist, re-clean substrate
Film is too thin to seeSpeed too high or resist too diluteUse 1000-2000 RPM for visible films
Resist pools instead of spreadingSpeed too low or resist too viscousIncrease speed or dilute resist slightly with water
Substrate flies off during spinInsufficient hold-downUse more tape or add vacuum retention
