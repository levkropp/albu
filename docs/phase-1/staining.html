Staining and Imaging

Making egg white patterns visible under the microscope using protein-specific stains.

Why Staining is Necessary

Crosslinked egg white is a thin (~100-900nm), mostly transparent protein film on glass. Without staining, the patterns are nearly invisible under a standard optical microscope. Protein-specific stains bind to the remaining resist and provide contrast against the clear glass substrate.

Iodine Stain (Primary Method)

Recommended ~2 minutes

Iodine-Potassium Iodide Stain

Materials

  • Iodine tincture 2% (from pharmacy)
  • Distilled water
  • Shallow dish

Procedure

  1. Prepare stain: add 3–5 drops of 2% iodine tincture to 50mL of distilled water in a shallow dish. The solution should be light amber.
  2. Immerse the developed slide for 30 seconds.
  3. Remove and rinse gently with distilled water from a squeeze bottle.
  4. Air dry.

Result: Remaining protein stains yellow-brown. Glass remains clear. The contrast is sufficient for imaging at 100–250x magnification.

Methylene Blue Stain (Alternative)

Alternative Higher contrast

Methylene Blue Protein Stain

Materials

  • Methylene blue solution (aquarium fish disease treatment, available at pet stores or Amazon)
  • Distilled water

Procedure

  1. Prepare stain: add 1 drop of methylene blue solution to 50mL of distilled water. The solution should be pale blue.
  2. Immerse the developed slide for 30 seconds.
  3. Rinse gently with distilled water.
  4. Air dry.

Result: Protein stains blue. Often provides higher contrast than iodine, especially on thinner films.

Try both stains on different slides from the same exposure batch. Different stains may perform better depending on film thickness and crosslinking degree. Both are reversible — restaining a slide with a different stain is possible after re-rinsing.

Microscope Imaging

Setup

  1. Place the stained slide on the microscope stage.
  2. Use transmitted light (bottom LED) for maximum contrast. The stained protein appears dark against a bright background.
  3. Start at the lowest magnification: 4x objective = 100x total. This gives a wide field of view (~4mm) to locate your patterns.
  4. Once you've found a pattern, switch to 10x objective = 250x total for detailed inspection of feature edges and quality.

Phone Photography

  1. Attach the phone adapter (included with BEBANG microscope) to the eyepiece.
  2. Align your phone camera lens with the eyepiece center. You should see the circular microscope field of view on your phone screen.
  3. Focus the microscope first, then fine-adjust the phone position for a centered, sharp image.
  4. Use the included wire shutter (plugs into phone headphone jack) for hands-free capture. This eliminates shake from tapping the screen.
  5. Take photos at each magnification and label them with the exposure parameters used.
Documentation tip: For each slide, photograph the same region at 100x and 250x. Include a reference feature (e.g., the edge of a known-width line) in each image so you can calculate actual feature sizes from the photos later.

What to Measure

For each exposure condition that produced a pattern, record:

  • Line width — measure the actual width of the stained line. Compare to the programmed width.
  • Edge quality — are the edges sharp or ragged? Is there a visible heat-affected zone?
  • Background cleanliness — is the unexposed area fully clear, or is there residual haze?
  • Contrast — how dark/intense is the stained pattern vs. the background?
  • Defects — cracks, pinholes, peeling, particles?

This data feeds directly into the exposure optimization and determines your resolution limit for the resolution test.
