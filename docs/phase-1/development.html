Water Development

Developing exposed egg white resist patterns using distilled water. No solvents, no specialty developers.

Materials

  • Distilled water
  • Shallow dish (petri dish, small plate, or plastic container)
  • Squeeze bottle with distilled water (for rinsing)
  • Canned air (optional, for drying)

Procedure

  1. Fill dish. Pour room-temperature distilled water into a shallow dish, deep enough to submerge the slide (~5mm).
  2. Immerse. Gently place the exposed slide into the water, film side up. Do not drop it — the splash can damage delicate patterns.
  3. Develop. Wait 30 seconds. Gently swirl the dish every 10 seconds to promote dissolution of soluble protein.
  4. Rinse. Remove the slide and rinse with fresh distilled water from a squeeze bottle, held at a shallow angle. The rinse removes loosened protein that's still clinging to the surface.
  5. Dry. Air dry by standing the slide upright, or gently blow with canned air (hold can upright to avoid propellant spray). Do not wipe — wiping will destroy the pattern.
Development time: The paper used 30 seconds. If patterns are incomplete (residual protein in areas that should be clear), try extending to 60 seconds or using gently warmed water (30-40°C). Over-development (>2 minutes) may attack even crosslinked areas.

What Happens During Development

Negative tone

Exposed regions contain crosslinked protein aggregates with water-inaccessible beta-sheet structure. These are insoluble in water and remain on the slide. Unexposed regions contain native, soluble protein that dissolves and washes away.

Result: Pattern matches the laser-exposed areas.

Positive tone

Exposed regions contain fragmented protein chains (small, water-soluble peptides). These dissolve readily. Unexposed regions contain glycerol-stabilized native protein, mildly pre-cured for adhesion, which remains on the slide.

Result: Pattern is the inverse of the laser-exposed areas.

Water Quality

Distilled water is recommended to avoid mineral deposits on the pattern. Tap water works for initial experiments but may leave residue that's visible under the microscope. Deionized water is ideal but not necessary for Phase 1 feature sizes.

Resist Stripping (for re-use of substrates)

If you need to clean a slide completely and start over:

  • Hot water soak: Immerse in near-boiling water for 5 minutes. Most crosslinked protein will soften and peel.
  • Enzyme digestion: Dissolve meat tenderizer (papain protease) in warm water (~1 teaspoon per 100mL). Soak the slide for 1–2 hours. The protease breaks down crosslinked protein.
  • Concentrated urea: The paper mentions 8M urea at 4°C overnight as an alternative. Urea is available at garden stores as fertilizer, but the meat tenderizer approach is more accessible.

Development Time Sweep

Optimal development time depends on film thickness, crosslinking degree, and water temperature. Run this sweep after finding working exposure parameters.

Use identically exposed slides (same power/speed/resist). Develop one slide per condition. Compare pattern quality, edge sharpness, and background cleanliness.

Expected result: Optimal development is likely 20–45 seconds at room temperature, matching the paper’s 30 second recommendation. Warm water may speed development but risks attacking crosslinked areas. Hot water will likely strip everything.
