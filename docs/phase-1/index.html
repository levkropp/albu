Phase 1: Complete Protocol

Step-by-step instructions for proving egg white photoresist at 450nm. Follow these in order. Each step includes the exact recipe, expected results, and troubleshooting.

Before you start: Ensure your CNC 3018 is assembled with the laser mounted and focused, your spin coater is calibrated to known RPM settings, and you have all consumables on hand. Wear laser safety goggles whenever the laser is powered.

Step 1: Preparing Egg White Resist

Negative Tone ~30 min prep

Negative Tone Resist (start here)

This produces a resist where exposed areas become insoluble. Simpler than positive tone — no glycerol, no precuring step.

Materials

  • 1 chicken egg (any grade, room temperature)
  • Coffee filter
  • Clean glass jar or beaker
  • Mason jar + hand vacuum pump (for degassing)
  • Riboflavin (vitamin B2) tablet, 100mg
  • Plastic pipette

Procedure

  1. Separate the egg. Crack the egg and cleanly separate the white from the yolk. Any yolk contamination will degrade the resist. Collect the white in a clean glass jar. Yield: ~20mL per egg.
  2. Filter. Pour the egg white through a coffee filter into a second clean jar. Let it drip through by gravity — do not squeeze. This removes the thick viscous chalazae and gel-like ovomucin layer. The paper (Figure S2) shows that unfiltered albumen gives poor patterns. Takes 5–10 minutes.
  3. Degas and partially dehydrate. Pour the filtered albumen into the mason jar. Connect the hand vacuum pump and pump until bubbles stop rising aggressively (~5 minutes). Continue pumping more gently until you observe a slight cloudiness at the edges of the liquid — this is protein "phasing out" and indicates sufficient dehydration. Stop here.

    Alternative if you have no vacuum pump: Let the jar sit covered in the fridge for 24 hours. Skim any foam from the surface before use.
  4. Add photosensitizer. Crush one 100mg riboflavin (B2) tablet into a fine powder. Add to the filtered albumen. Stir gently for 2–3 minutes until dissolved. The liquid will take on a slight yellow tint. Target concentration: ~1–5 mg/mL. Don't worry about exact dosing — you'll optimize via the exposure dose matrix.

    Why riboflavin: The original paper used 254nm UV. Our laser is 450nm. Riboflavin has a strong absorption peak at 445–450nm and acts as a Type II photosensitizer, generating singlet oxygen that crosslinks proteins. It's naturally present in egg white in trace amounts; we're boosting the concentration.
  5. Store. Cap the jar and refrigerate. The resist is stable for approximately 1 week. Label with the date. One egg yields enough resist for ~65 slides.
Positive Tone Same prep + glycerol

Positive Tone Resist (attempt after negative works)

In positive tone, exposed areas become soluble and wash away. The key additive is glycerol at a 10:1 ratio.

Follow the same procedure as negative tone, then:

  1. Measure 10mL of filtered, degassed, riboflavin-doped albumen.
  2. Add 1mL of food-grade glycerol (10:1 volume ratio, albumen:glycerol).
  3. Mix gently to avoid introducing bubbles.
  4. The glycerol inhibits protein aggregation and promotes chain fragmentation under exposure. Higher glycerol ratios weaken substrate adhesion (paper Figure S6). Lower ratios fail to prevent aggregation. 10:1 is the sweet spot.
Critical: The glycerol ratio matters. More than 1:10 = poor adhesion, film peels off during development. Less than 0.5:10 = reverts to negative tone behavior. Stick to 1:10 initially.

Step 2: Spin Coating

Materials

  • Prepared egg white resist (from Step 1)
  • Glass microscope slides (clean)
  • Isopropyl alcohol 99%
  • Spin coater (calibrated)
  • Plastic pipettes

Procedure

  1. Clean the substrate. Wipe the glass slide with isopropyl alcohol using a lint-free wipe or clean paper towel. Allow to air dry completely (~30 seconds). A clean surface is essential for uniform coating.
  2. Mount on spin coater. Place the slide on the spin coater chuck. Secure with double-sided tape (center only) or vacuum if your coater has it. Verify the slide is centered and level.
  3. Dispense resist. Using a plastic pipette, deposit approximately 300μL (~6 drops) of egg white resist onto the center of the slide. Avoid touching the pipette tip to the slide surface.
  4. Spin. Start the spin coater at your target RPM. Hold for 30 seconds.

    For the initial experiments, spin coat 5 slides at different speeds to map the thickness:
    SlideSpeedExpected ThicknessNotes
    A1000 RPM~900nmThick, easy to see, most forgiving
    B2000 RPM~450nmStandard for gate dielectric (Phase 2)
    C3000 RPM~300nmStandard for photoresist
    D4000 RPM~200nmThin
    E6000 RPM~100nmThinnest achievable
  5. Dry. Let the coated slide air dry at room temperature for 5 minutes, or place on a hot plate at 40°C for 2 minutes. The film should be smooth, mostly transparent, with a possible faint color from the riboflavin.

How to verify uniformity: Tilt the slide under a light source. Thin-film interference colors (rainbow tint) indicate a thin, somewhat uniform film. Consistent color across the surface means good uniformity. Splotchy or streaked areas indicate a problem with the spin coater balance or resist viscosity.

Step 3: Laser Exposure

Safety: Always wear OD6+ laser safety goggles when the laser is powered. Never look directly at the laser spot, even with goggles. Ensure the CNC enclosure shields stray reflections.

Step 4: Water Development

  1. Fill a shallow dish (petri dish or small plate) with room-temperature distilled water.
  2. Gently immerse the exposed slide, film side up.
  3. Wait 30 seconds. Gently swirl the dish every 10 seconds.
  4. Remove the slide. Rinse gently with fresh distilled water from a squeeze bottle.
  5. Air dry, or gently blow dry with canned air (hold the can upright to avoid propellant spray).

What to expect

Negative tone (no glycerol): The laser-exposed lines should remain on the slide. Unexposed areas should have washed away. The remaining lines are crosslinked protein — water-insoluble.

Positive tone (with glycerol): The laser-exposed lines should have washed away. Unexposed areas remain. The exposed protein was fragmented into water-soluble fragments.

Step 5: Staining and Imaging

The remaining egg white film is mostly transparent on glass. Staining makes the patterns visible under the microscope.

Iodine stain (primary method)

  1. Prepare stain: 3–5 drops of 2% iodine tincture in 50mL distilled water in a shallow dish.
  2. Immerse the developed slide for 30 seconds.
  3. Remove and rinse gently with distilled water.
  4. Air dry.
  5. The remaining protein stains yellow-brown. Glass remains clear.

Methylene blue stain (alternative)

  1. Prepare stain: 1 drop of methylene blue solution (aquarium treatment concentration) in 50mL distilled water.
  2. Immerse for 30 seconds. Rinse. Dry.
  3. Protein stains blue. Glass remains clear.

Imaging

  1. Place the stained slide on the microscope stage.
  2. Start at the lowest magnification (4x objective, 100x total) to find your patterns.
  3. Switch to 10x objective (250x total) for detailed inspection.
  4. Use the phone adapter to photograph through the eyepiece. Align the phone camera with the eyepiece, hold steady, and capture.
  5. Document: which power/speed combinations produced clean lines? Which failed? Measure line widths if possible using the eyepiece reticle or by comparison to a known dimension on the slide.

Success Criteria

Phase 1 is successful if:

  • You can produce visible, stained egg white patterns on glass after water development
  • The patterns correspond to the laser-written design (not random artifacts)
  • You achieve resolved features at ≤100μm
  • Both positive and negative tone modes work (or at minimum, negative tone works)

Document everything: photos, exposure parameters, development times, stain method, any failures and what caused them. This data feeds directly into Phase 2 process optimization.

What's next: Once Phase 1 succeeds, Phase 2 begins immediately. The first transistor (ZnO TFT on glass) uses the same spin coater, the same laser, and the same egg white — now as a gate dielectric instead of a photoresist. The transition is seamless.
