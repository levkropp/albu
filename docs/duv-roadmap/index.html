DUV Roadmap: The Schwarzschild Stepper

A Schwarzschild reflective objective + surplus KrF excimer laser = 500nm features at 1/100th the cost of a conventional DUV stepper. This page documents the long-term roadmap for KOSL's DUV lithography capability.

This is a future roadmap. The DUV stepper depends on completing Phases 1–4 (the Albu process) first. The KOSL machine at 10–50μm writes the photomasks that the DUV stepper reduces to 500nm. Bootstrap.

The Key Insight: Reflective Optics

Production DUV steppers—the machines ASML, Nikon, and Canon sell for $5–50M—use 20–30 fused silica lens elements in their projection optics. They need that many because they are trying to achieve NA=0.7 over a 26×33mm exposure field using a single optical material (fused silica is the only glass that transmits well at 248nm). Chromatic aberration, field curvature, distortion, coma, and astigmatism must all be corrected simultaneously, and with only one refractive index to work with, you need a lot of corrective surfaces. Each element must be polished to sub-nanometer surface figure. The lens column alone costs millions.

But there is another way. Schwarzschild reflective objectives use two spherical mirrors—one concave, one convex—in a concentric arrangement. Mirrors have zero chromatic aberration at any wavelength, because reflection does not depend on refractive index. A two-mirror Schwarzschild design also corrects spherical aberration and coma by geometry. The result is a diffraction-limited objective that works from deep UV through infrared, with just two optical surfaces instead of thirty.

These are not exotic lab curiosities. Thorlabs sells Schwarzschild reflective objectives off the shelf for $2–5K:

  • 15x magnification, NA=0.3, designed for 200nm–20μm wavelength range
  • 40x magnification, NA=0.5, for higher resolution at smaller field
  • Standard microscope thread mount, integrates with commercial stages

At 248nm (KrF excimer) with NA=0.3, the Rayleigh resolution limit is:

R = k1 * λ / NA
R = 0.6 * 248nm / 0.3
R ≈ 500nm

With optical proximity correction (OPC):
R = 0.4 * 248nm / 0.3
R ≈ 330nm

The field of view is small—roughly 0.5–1mm diameter—compared to the 26×33mm field of a production stepper. But that is what step-and-repeat is for: expose one field, move the stage, expose the next. A 10×10mm die takes 100–400 shots at ~1mm field. At 1 shot per second, that is under 7 minutes per die. Slower than a production scanner, but perfectly adequate for prototyping and small-batch fabrication.

This is not a new idea. Bell Labs used Schwarzschild objectives for early EUV lithography research in the 1980s and 1990s. Kinoshita et al. demonstrated 50nm features with a Schwarzschild reflector at 13nm wavelength. The concept is proven—it just was never commercialized as a lithography product because the semiconductor industry scaled toward high-throughput scanners with enormous fields. For a research lab or small fab printing a few dozen dies, the Schwarzschild approach is ideal.

System Architecture

The full DUV stepper is a straightforward optical system with six major components:

KrF Excimer Laser (248nm, surplus $5-15K)
    → Beam Homogenizer (fly's eye or rod integrator, $1-2K)
    → Condenser Lens (fused silica singlet, $500)
    → Chrome-on-Quartz Mask (made by KOSL machine or purchased)
    → Schwarzschild Reflective Objective, 15x, NA=0.3 ($3-5K)
    → Wafer on Precision XY Stage ($10-50K)
    → Alignment Camera

Resolution:     350-500nm
Field:          0.5-1mm (step and repeat)
Reduction:      15:1
Dose:           ~50-200 mJ/cm² (resist dependent)
Throughput:     ~2-10 dies/hour (10x10mm die)

Component Details

  • KrF Excimer Laser (248nm): Surplus units from decommissioned industrial systems are available for $5–15K on eBay and surplus equipment dealers. Lambda Physik and Cymer models are common. These produce 10–200mJ per pulse at 100–500Hz, far more energy than needed for resist exposure. Key maintenance: gas refills (Kr/F&sub2;/Ne mix) every few thousand hours.
  • Beam Homogenizer: The raw excimer beam has a non-uniform "top hat with hot spots" profile. A fly's-eye lens array or rod integrator homogenizes the beam to uniform intensity across the mask field. Critical for uniform exposure dose. Can be fabricated from commercial microlens arrays ($500–2K).
  • Condenser: A single fused silica plano-convex lens images the homogenized beam onto the mask plane. At 248nm, fused silica (Corning 7980 or equivalent) transmits >90%. Off-the-shelf from Edmund Optics or Thorlabs.
  • Photomask: Chrome-on-quartz, the industry standard. At 15:1 reduction, a 500nm wafer feature requires only a 7.5μm feature on the mask. The KOSL 10MT5000 Auto writes 10μm features—it can bootstrap its own DUV masks. For finer mask features, electron-beam mask writing services are available ($500–2K per mask).
  • Schwarzschild Objective: The Thorlabs LMM-15X-UVV (15x, NA=0.3) or similar. Mounts with standard RMS or M26 threads. The objective simultaneously demagnifies (15:1) and focuses the mask pattern onto the wafer.
  • Precision XY Stage: This is the most variable-cost component. A $10K stage from Thorlabs or Newport provides 1μm positioning accuracy with 50×50mm travel—adequate for 500nm lithography. A $50K air-bearing stage with interferometric feedback provides sub-100nm positioning for tighter overlay requirements.
The bootstrap loop: 15:1 reduction means a 500nm wafer feature needs 7.5μm on the mask. The KOSL 10MT5000 Auto writes 10μm features. It can write its own DUV masks. The DUV stepper then prints 500nm features on wafers. Those wafers can be used to build better control electronics for the next generation of KOSL machines. This is a self-reinforcing capability ladder.

Transistor Density at 500nm

At 500nm minimum feature size with 2–3 metal layers, the achievable transistor densities are:

TypeDensityNotes
Logic (placed & routed)~20,000 transistors/mm²Standard-cell automated layout with routing overhead
SRAM (6T with peripherals)~0.9 KB/mm²6-transistor cell, sense amps, row/column decoders included
Mask ROM~25 KB/mm²Single-transistor cell, via-programmed, much denser than SRAM

These numbers come from actual 0.5μm-era datasheets and die photos. Logic density includes routing channels, power rails, and standard-cell overhead—not just raw gate count. SRAM density includes the peripheral circuits (decoders, sense amplifiers, I/O) that a real memory block requires. These are conservative, realistic estimates for what you can actually build.

The Automotive Opportunity

The global chip shortage of 2020–2023 was not a leading-edge problem. TSMC's 5nm and 3nm lines kept running. The shortage was overwhelmingly concentrated in mature nodes: 40nm, 90nm, 130nm, 180nm, and 350–500nm. These are the nodes that produce automotive MCUs, power management ICs, sensor interfaces, and industrial controllers—the chips that every car, appliance, and factory needs dozens of.

The root cause was structural:

  • Fab capacity at mature nodes is concentrated in a few facilities, mostly in Taiwan, Japan, and China. When demand surged post-COVID, there was no spare capacity to absorb it.
  • No one is building new fabs for 500nm. The economics of a $1–5B fab don't work for $0.50 chips. Existing capacity slowly shrinks as old fabs are repurposed or decommissioned.
  • Automotive qualification cycles are 2–5 years. You can't just move an automotive MCU to a new fab or new node—it must be requalified through AEC-Q100, and the automakers won't accept it without years of reliability data on the new process.

Automotive MCUs at 500nm are still manufactured and sold today. NXP, Infineon, Renesas, and Microchip all have active product lines on 350–500nm processes. These are not legacy parts being phased out—they are current-production, actively designed chips. The automotive industry will need 500nm-class chips for decades.

The Case for Domestic Fabrication

An open-source, domestically fabricable automotive MCU addresses a real supply chain vulnerability:

  • Design: Open-source RISC-V core (e.g., SERV, PicoRV32, or Ibex) + open-source CAN controller + open-source ADC. Full RTL available, no IP licensing fees.
  • Fabrication: KOSL Schwarzschild stepper + open-source process. Any university lab, community fab, or small company can set up a line.
  • Qualification: Start with non-safety-critical applications (body electronics, infotainment, seat controllers) where AEC-Q100 qualification is simpler.
  • Scale: A single stepper running two shifts can produce ~500 wafers/month. At 100 dies per 150mm wafer, that's 50,000 MCUs/month—enough for a regional automotive supplier.
Funding angle: This is a defensible national security and supply chain resilience argument. The CHIPS Act, NSF, and DoD all have funding programs for domestic semiconductor capability. A $200K DUV stepper that can produce automotive MCUs is exactly the kind of distributed manufacturing capability these programs aim to create. The grant practically writes itself: open-source chip design + open-source fabrication tool + domestic manufacturing = supply chain resilience.

The KOSL DUV Product Vision

The Schwarzschild stepper is not just a research tool—it is a product.

Target Price: $200–300K

This is approximately one-third the cost of a used ASML PAS 5500 (the cheapest used DUV stepper on the market, typically $500K–1M for a working unit). But unlike a used ASML:

  • No export controls. The Schwarzschild stepper uses commercially available components. No ITAR, no EAR, no Wassenaar restrictions. Ship it anywhere.
  • No vendor lock-in. Open-source design, open-source control software, standard components. The customer can maintain and modify it themselves.
  • No cleanroom required for the tool itself. The stepper operates in ambient atmosphere (the wafer processing needs cleanroom conditions, but the stepper does not need a massive subfab).
  • Footprint: Fits on a single optical table (~1.2m × 2.4m). An ASML PAS 5500 needs a dedicated room with raised floor and vibration isolation.

Target Customers

  • Universities: Teaching labs, research groups in MEMS, photonics, microfluidics, analog IC design. Most universities cannot afford or maintain a used ASML. A $200K stepper with open-source documentation changes the equation.
  • Community fabs / makerspaces: The semiconductor equivalent of a community workshop. Groups like SkyWater's open-source PDK community, Libre Silicon, and university cleanrooms.
  • Small companies: Startups doing custom ASIC prototyping, MEMS sensors, photonic devices, or specialized analog circuits where 500nm is adequate and fast turnaround matters more than density.
  • Government / defense labs: Secure, domestic, trusted fabrication of radiation-hardened or tamper-evident ICs. No foreign supply chain dependencies.

Naming Convention

KOSL DUV product names follow the pattern:

KOSL [resolution]NM[transistor_capacity] DUV

Examples:
  KOSL 500NM250K DUV   — 500nm resolution, 250K transistor capability (5×5mm die)
  KOSL 500NM1M DUV     — 500nm resolution, 1M transistor capability (10×10mm die)
  KOSL 350NM4M DUV     — 350nm resolution (with OPC), 4M transistors (20×20mm die)

The transistor capacity in the name indicates the maximum single-die complexity the stage travel and alignment system can support. Resolution is the minimum half-pitch the objective and illumination can achieve.

Historical Context

500nm (0.5μm) is not a retro technology node. It is the node that built the computing infrastructure of the 1990s. Here is what the semiconductor industry produced at this exact feature size:

ChipYearTransistorsDie SizeNotes
Intel Pentium (P54C) 1994 3.3M 163 mm² Ran Windows 95. Superscalar, dual-pipeline, 64-bit data bus. The chip that brought the internet to desktops.
PowerPC 603 1993 1.6M 85 mm² First PowerPC chip in Macintosh computers. Apple/IBM/Motorola AIM alliance. Powered Macs until the Intel transition.
ARM7TDMI 1994 72K (core) tiny The most licensed processor architecture in history. Powered the Game Boy Advance, iPod, and thousands of embedded systems. Still in production today.
MIPS R4600 1994 1.35M 77 mm² Low-cost 64-bit MIPS processor. Powered SGI Indy workstations. Used in early Cisco routers.

500nm is not retro. It's the node that built the computing infrastructure of the 1990s. With modern open-source HDL and synthesis tools, we can design chips more capable than those originals. The 1990s designers worked with proprietary, expensive EDA tools on Sun workstations. Today, Yosys + OpenROAD + Verilator run on a laptop and produce results that rival commercial synthesis. The RTL for a Pentium-class processor is available on GitHub. The only missing piece was the fabrication tool.

That is what the Schwarzschild stepper provides: the last mile from design to silicon, at a price point accessible to universities, startups, and community fabs. Not 3nm. Not 7nm. The node where most of the world's useful chips are still made. The node that matters.

The path from here: Phase 1 (egg white photoresist) → Phase 2 (first transistor) → Phase 3 (patterning with KOSL machine at 10μm) → Phase 4 (integrated circuits) → DUV stepper (500nm). Each phase builds on the last. Each phase produces something useful. The DUV stepper is the destination, but every waypoint is a real product.
