How Egg White Resist Works

The protein chemistry behind using chicken egg white as a photoresist for micro/nanofabrication.

Background

Egg white (albumen) is approximately 90% water and 10% protein by weight. The dominant protein is ovalbumin (~62% of total protein), a globular protein that undergoes structural changes upon exposure to UV or electron-beam radiation.

This property was first systematically exploited for lithography by Jiang et al. (2017), who demonstrated both positive and negative tone patterning at sub-micron resolution using egg white as a resist material.

Negative Tone: Protein Aggregation

When globular proteins in egg white are exposed to energetic radiation (UV, electron beam, or in our case, 450nm laser via riboflavin photosensitization), they undergo denaturation and aggregation:

  1. Unfolding: The radiation disrupts the native globular conformation, exposing hydrophobic core regions.
  2. Beta-sheet formation: Unfolded proteins reorganize into intermolecular beta-sheet structures, driven by hydrogen bonding between exposed backbone amide groups.
  3. Crosslinking: Disulfide bonds form between cysteine residues on adjacent protein chains. Hydrophobic interactions and electrostatic forces further stabilize the aggregate.
  4. Insolubility: The resulting aggregated protein network is water-insoluble. The exposed beta-sheet structure is "water-inaccessible."

After exposure, rinsing with water removes the unexposed (still-soluble) protein, leaving behind the crosslinked pattern. This is negative tone behavior: exposed regions remain.

Positive Tone: Protein Fragmentation

Adding glycerol to the albumen (10:1 v/v ratio) switches the resist to positive tone. Glycerol has three effects:

  1. Lubrication: Glycerol's hydroxyl groups form hydrogen bonds with protein chains, partially replacing native interprotein interactions. This weakens protein-protein attractions.
  2. Stabilization: Glycerol increases the thermodynamic stability of the native (folded) protein conformation through preferential hydration, reducing the tendency to aggregate.
  3. Inhibition of assembly: Glycerol slows the formation of beta-sheet fibrillar structures that would otherwise lock proteins into an insoluble aggregate.

With aggregation suppressed, the dominant radiation effect becomes chain fragmentation (peptide bond scission). The fragmented proteins are small, water-soluble species that wash away during development. Unexposed regions (where protein remains intact and glycerol-stabilized) stay on the substrate.

A two-step exposure is used for positive tone:

  1. Low-intensity pre-cure: A gentle, uniform exposure initiates mild aggregation across the entire film, securing it to the substrate. Without this step, the water development would strip the entire film.
  2. High-intensity patterned exposure: Through a photomask or by laser direct-write, the high-dose exposure fragments the protein in the exposed regions. These regions wash away in water.

Why Egg White Works as a Dielectric

Crosslinked egg white is not just a resist — it's also a functional gate dielectric (Chang et al. 2011). After crosslinking:

  • Dielectric constant κ ≈ 5–6 (higher than SiO2 at 3.9)
  • Breakdown field: ~2–4 MV/cm (sufficient for transistors operating at 5–10V)
  • Low leakage current: The hydrophobic beta-sheet network resists ionic conduction
  • Smooth surface: ~5nm RMS roughness after crosslinking

This dual functionality (resist + dielectric) means the same material and the same deposition process serves two purposes in our transistor fabrication flow.

Film Thickness Control

Egg white film thickness follows the standard spin-coating relationship: faster spin = thinner film. From the paper (Figure S11):

Spin Speed (RPM)Thickness (nm)Typical Use
1000~900Thick resist, easy to see
2000~450Gate dielectric for TFTs
3000~300Standard resist thickness
4000~200Thin resist
6000~100Minimum achievable thickness

Diluting the albumen with water before spin-coating can produce even thinner films. This has not been characterized in the literature but is worth exploring for ultra-thin gate dielectrics.
