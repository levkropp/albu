Phase 4: SERV RISC-V CPU

The endgame — fabricating SERV, the world's smallest RISC-V CPU, on a home-built CMOS process. ~6,000 transistors on a single die, written with egg white photoresist.

What Is SERV?

SERV is a bit-serial RISC-V CPU designed by Olof Kindgren. It implements the RV32I base integer instruction set by processing one bit at a time, trading speed for minimal area. It is fully open source.

PropertyValue
Instruction setRV32I (base integer)
ArchitectureBit-serial (1 bit per cycle)
FPGA size~1,000 LUTs
CMOS transistor count~4,000–8,000 (4–8 per gate)
LicenseISC (fully open source)

SERV's serial architecture makes it uniquely suited to home fabrication: it minimizes transistor count at the cost of clock cycles per instruction (32 cycles for a 32-bit operation). For a proof-of-concept CPU, transistor count matters far more than speed.

Die Size Estimates

At 10μm minimum feature size (Machine 3's target resolution), each transistor occupies significantly more area than in commercial processes. But SERV is small enough that it still fits on a single die.

A 7mm × 7mm die fits comfortably within the Machine 3 exposure field. The routing overhead (interconnect wiring between transistors) dominates the die area, as expected at this feature size.

CMOS Process Requirements

A CPU requires both n-type and p-type transistors for CMOS logic. Two process options are available from Phase 2:

Option A: Oxide TFT CMOS (Glass Substrate)

  • NMOS: ZnO thin-film transistors
  • PMOS: Cu₂O thin-film transistors
  • Both on glass, patterned with vinegar etch
  • Egg white gate dielectric for both

Option B: Laser-Doped Silicon CMOS

  • NMOS: Phosphoric acid laser doping (n+ source/drain in p-type Si)
  • PMOS: Boric acid laser doping (p+ source/drain in n-type Si, or n-well in p-type Si)
  • Requires both dopant types and potentially a twin-well process
  • Higher performance but more complex

Layer Count

Minimum 6–8 mask layers for a functional CMOS process:

  1. Well — defines n-well or p-well regions
  2. Active — defines transistor channel areas
  3. Gate — defines gate electrodes
  4. Contact — opens vias to source/drain/gate
  5. Metal 1 — first routing layer
  6. Via — connects Metal 1 to Metal 2
  7. Metal 2 — second routing layer

Each layer is aligned and exposed by Machine 3 using the software alignment system described in Phase 3.

Yield Analysis

Yield is the probability that every transistor on the die works. With thousands of transistors, even small per-device failure rates compound dramatically.

Per-Transistor Yield6,000 TransistorsDie Yield
99.0%e-60×0.01~55%
99.5%e-60×0.005~74%
99.9%e-60×0.001~94%
99.99%e-60×0.0001~99.4%

The target: defect density below 10/cm² across the die area. At 99% per-transistor yield, roughly half of all dice work — marginal but potentially viable with multiple attempts. At 99.9%, yield is comfortable. Getting from 99% to 99.9% is the central engineering challenge.

This is the hardest part of the entire project. No amount of clever architecture can compensate for poor yield. Every process step — coating uniformity, exposure dose control, development, etching, contact quality — must be optimized to near-perfection before attempting a full SERV die.

Honest Challenges

Phase 4 is the most ambitious goal in the Albu project. These are the hard problems that must be solved:

Alignment Tolerance

At 10μm feature sizes, layer-to-layer alignment must be better than ~3μm (<1/3 of minimum feature). Machine 3's camera-based alignment targets 50nm, which is sufficient in theory — but thermal drift, stage hysteresis, and substrate warping can all degrade real-world performance.

Yield

The fundamental challenge. Every defect source must be identified and eliminated: particles in the resist, pinholes in the gate dielectric, incomplete etching, contact resistance variation, film thickness non-uniformity. Statistical process control across thousands of devices is required.

Multi-Layer Process Complexity

A 6–8 layer process means the substrate goes through coating, exposure, development, and etching 6–8 times. Each cycle introduces opportunities for contamination, misalignment, and damage to previous layers. Process integration — ensuring each step is compatible with all prior steps — is a major engineering effort.

PMOS Characterization

Cu₂O p-type TFTs are less mature than ZnO n-type TFTs. Mobility is lower, threshold voltage is harder to control, and on/off ratios are typically worse. Balancing NMOS and PMOS characteristics for functional CMOS inverters requires careful process tuning.

Interconnect Routing

6,000 transistors need to be wired together. At 10μm feature size, two metal layers may not provide enough routing density. Via reliability (electrical connection between Metal 1 and Metal 2) is critical — a single open via can kill a signal path.

The honest truth: Phase 4 may take years of iteration. The intermediate milestones (inverter, ring oscillator, flip-flop) are valuable demonstrations in their own right. Even a working 4-bit adder fabricated at home would be a significant achievement. SERV is the north star, not a short-term deliverable.
