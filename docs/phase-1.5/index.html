Phase 1.5: Automated DLP Lithography

Upgrading the CNC 3018 from manual laser direct-write to automated DLP maskless projection lithography. This intermediate phase transforms the Phase 1 proof-of-concept into a semi-automated fab tool — the basis for the KO-1 Lite ($4,999 early adopter product).

Why Phase 1.5?

Phase 1 proves the resist works. Using the CNC 3018’s 5W laser in direct-write mode, we demonstrate pattern transfer at ~100μm resolution — enough to confirm the photochemistry, the develop process, and the basic workflow. But 100μm features are too coarse for anything beyond the simplest test structures, and every step is manual: hand-pipetting resist, manually focusing the laser, visually checking development by eye.

Phase 1.5 bridges the gap between “it works” and “it’s a process.” Three critical upgrades transform the CNC 3018 into a semi-automated lithography tool:

  • DLP maskless exposure (5–25μm): A used DLP projector replaces the scanning laser for pattern exposure. Instead of raster-scanning a single point, the DLP flashes an entire mask image onto the wafer in one shot. Resolution improves from ~100μm to 5–50μm depending on reduction optics. Exposure time drops from minutes (scanning) to seconds (flood).
  • Automated wafer handling: A gantry-mounted vacuum pickup nozzle moves the wafer between stations — spin coater, hot plate, exposure zone, develop tray, cassette — without human hands touching the substrate. This eliminates the single largest source of contamination and mishandling.
  • Automated development: Solenoid-controlled fill, drain, rinse, and dry cycle replaces hand-dipping. Timing is precise and repeatable. No more over-developing or under-rinsing.

The resulting machine is the KO-1 Lite — a desktop lithography tool sold to early adopters for $4,999 CAD. Revenue from KO-1 Lite sales funds Phase 3 development (the full KO-1 with precision stages and HEPA enclosure).

Phase 1.5 is not a detour. Every component added here (DLP projector, vacuum pickup, develop station, Arduino GPIO control) carries forward directly into Phase 3 and the KO-1. Nothing is throwaway work.

DLP Projector Setup

The Casio XJ-M140 is a laser/LED hybrid DLP projector. Its blue channel uses a 455nm laser diode — not a broadband lamp. This is critical: 455nm is close enough to the 405nm sensitivity peak of most photoresists to achieve exposure, and the laser source is far more spatially coherent than an LED, enabling sharp imaging through reduction optics.

Display Configuration

  • Input: HDMI from the control computer. The projector appears as a second monitor.
  • Pattern generation: Python script renders mask patterns as full-screen images on the projector output. Blue-only images (R=0, G=0, B=max) ensure only the 455nm laser diode fires — the red and green LED sources remain off, preventing unwanted broadband exposure.
  • Resolution: Native 1024×768 XGA. Each DLP micromirror pixel is approximately 13μm at the DMD plane.
  • Exposure control: Display a white (blue) pattern for the calibrated exposure time, then display black to stop. Alternatively, use the projector’s built-in shutter if available.

Mounting and Optics

The projector mounts on a shelf or bracket above the CNC bed, pointed downward at the EXPOSE station. Between the projector lens and the wafer, reduction optics demagnify the projected image:

OptionReductionPixel on WaferField SizeBest For
A: 5x objective5:1~50μm~6.6 × 5mmAlbu-4 CPU at 25–50μm (die fits in 1–2 shots)
B: 2x lens2:1~6.5μm~13 × 10mmSERV at 10–13μm (die fits in 1 shot)
C: 1:1 (no reduction)1:1~13μm~13 × 10mmLargest field, simplest optics, no objective needed

For the KO-1 Lite targeting the Albu 50M process (50μm features), Option A (5x reduction) is the default. The condenser lens (aspheric, 25mm focal length) collimates the diverging DLP output into a parallel beam, which the 5x microscope objective then focuses onto the wafer at 5:1 demagnification. The Albu-4 CPU die (~5mm) fits within the 6.6×5mm field, so the entire die is exposed in a single flash — no stitching required.

For users targeting SERV at 10–13μm, swapping to Option B (2x reduction lens) or Option C (direct 1:1 projection) provides finer resolution at the cost of requiring more precise alignment.

No lamp modification needed. Unlike older mercury-lamp projectors that require UV filter removal, the Casio XJ-M140’s laser diode outputs 455nm directly. Just send blue-only images and you get a clean narrowband exposure source.

Automated Process Flow

A Python orchestration script controls the full layer cycle. The script communicates with the CNC gantry (G-code over serial) and the Arduino Mega (relay commands over serial) to coordinate all station operations.

One Layer Cycle

  1. PICK from cassette: Gantry moves to cassette position. Z lowers to wafer slot height. Vacuum pump activates, solenoid routes vacuum to suction cup. Cup seals on wafer. Z raises. Gantry translates to spin coater. (~15 seconds)
  2. COAT on spin coater: Gantry places wafer on spin chuck (vacuum off, vent to release). Resist dispenser deposits ~300μL onto center of wafer. Spin motor ramps to target RPM (3000–6000 depending on desired thickness) for 30 seconds. Wafer remains on chuck while spin decelerates. (~45 seconds)
  3. BAKE on hot plate: Gantry picks up coated wafer, places on hot plate station. Hot plate holds 60°C for 2 minutes (soft bake to drive off solvent and improve resist adhesion). (~2 minutes 15 seconds including transfer)
  4. EXPOSE under DLP: Gantry picks up baked wafer, places on expose station sub-stage (micrometer-adjustable platform). For layer 1, wafer is simply centered. For layer 2+, USB camera captures image through beam splitter, software detects alignment marks from previous layer, servo-driven micrometers adjust wafer XY position. Python script displays mask pattern on DLP projector (blue-only, full screen). Exposure time: 20–180 seconds depending on resist sensitivity and DLP intensity. (~30 seconds for layer 1, ~90 seconds for aligned layers)
  5. DEVELOP in tray: Gantry picks up exposed wafer, places in develop tray. Solenoid valve opens to fill tray with DI water (or developer solution). Soak for 30 seconds. Drain valve opens. Peristaltic pump sprays fresh rinse water for 10 seconds. Air nozzle blow-dries the wafer for 15 seconds. (~75 seconds)
  6. INSPECT under camera: Gantry picks up developed wafer, positions under USB camera. Software captures image and checks pattern quality — contrast, edge sharpness, defect count. Results logged. If pattern fails quality check, wafer is flagged for manual review. (~20 seconds)
  7. RETURN to cassette: Gantry places wafer back in cassette slot. Vacuum off, vent to release. Layer complete. (~15 seconds)

Total time per layer: ~5–6 minutes. A 6-layer CPU process (gate oxide, gate metal, source/drain implant, contact holes, metal interconnect, passivation) completes in approximately 35 minutes with no human intervention after loading the blank wafer into the cassette.

Pseudocode

def run_layer(layer_num, mask_image, expose_time_s):
    # 1. Pick wafer from cassette
    gantry.move_to(CASSETTE_POS)
    gantry.lower_z(CASSETTE_SLOT_HEIGHT[layer_num])
    vacuum.activate()
    time.sleep(0.5)  # seal time
    gantry.raise_z()

    # 2. Coat
    gantry.move_to(SPIN_COATER_POS)
    gantry.lower_z(SPIN_COATER_HEIGHT)
    vacuum.release()
    dispenser.dispense(volume_uL=300)
    spin_motor.run(rpm=4000, duration_s=30)
    time.sleep(5)  # deceleration

    # 3. Bake
    vacuum.activate()  # pick up from spin coater
    gantry.raise_z()
    gantry.move_to(HOT_PLATE_POS)
    gantry.lower_z(HOT_PLATE_HEIGHT)
    vacuum.release()
    hot_plate.set_temp(60)
    time.sleep(120)  # 2-minute soft bake

    # 4. Expose
    vacuum.activate()  # pick up from hot plate
    gantry.raise_z()
    gantry.move_to(EXPOSE_POS)
    gantry.lower_z(EXPOSE_HEIGHT)
    vacuum.release()

    if layer_num > 1:
        img = camera.capture()
        offset = alignment.detect_marks(img)
        micrometers.adjust(offset)
        img2 = camera.capture()
        alignment.verify(img2)  # confirm <5um error

    dlp.display(mask_image)
    time.sleep(expose_time_s)
    dlp.blank()

    # 5. Develop
    vacuum.activate()
    gantry.raise_z()
    gantry.move_to(DEVELOP_POS)
    gantry.lower_z(DEVELOP_HEIGHT)
    vacuum.release()
    fill_valve.open()
    time.sleep(3)  # fill tray
    fill_valve.close()
    time.sleep(30)  # develop soak
    drain_valve.open()
    time.sleep(5)
    drain_valve.close()
    rinse_pump.run(duration_s=10)
    air_nozzle.blow(duration_s=15)

    # 6. Inspect
    vacuum.activate()
    gantry.raise_z()
    gantry.move_to(CAMERA_POS)
    result = camera.inspect_pattern()
    log_result(layer_num, result)

    # 7. Return to cassette
    gantry.move_to(CASSETTE_POS)
    gantry.lower_z(CASSETTE_SLOT_HEIGHT[layer_num])
    vacuum.release()

    return result

Alignment System

Layer-to-layer alignment is the most challenging automation problem in Phase 1.5. The DLP defines the pattern, but the wafer must be positioned so that each new layer registers precisely with the previous layers.

Optical Setup

  • Camera placement: USB camera (5MP) is co-aligned with the DLP projection axis. Two approaches: (A) beam splitter on the DLP optical axis so the camera sees exactly what the DLP illuminates, or (B) offset camera with a known, calibrated XY offset from the DLP center. Option A is more accurate; Option B is simpler to build.
  • Illumination: Ring LED or off-axis LED provides illumination for camera inspection independent of the DLP. The camera needs to see the wafer surface clearly even when the DLP is not exposing.

Alignment Procedure

  • Layer 1: No alignment needed. The wafer is simply centered on the expose station. The first mask pattern defines the reference coordinate system.
  • Layer 2+: The camera captures an image of the wafer. OpenCV template matching (or contour detection) locates cross-shaped alignment marks printed in the previous layer. The software calculates the XY offset and rotation between the detected marks and their expected positions in the new mask.
  • Correction: Two SG90 micro servos push against micrometer heads mounted on the expose station sub-stage. Each micrometer has a return spring. The software commands a servo angle, which translates to a micrometer displacement, which shifts the wafer in X or Y. Resolution: approximately 2μm per servo step.
  • Verification: After adjustment, the camera re-captures and the software confirms the alignment error is within tolerance. If not, the adjust-verify loop repeats (up to 5 iterations) before flagging for manual intervention.

Capability

The KO-1 Lite runs the Albu 50M process: 50μm minimum features, suitable for circuits of approximately 100–500 transistors. This is enough for simple logic gates, ring oscillators, shift registers, small counters, and basic analog circuits (amplifiers, current mirrors). Not enough for a CPU — that requires the full KO-1 — but enough to learn semiconductor fabrication and build real working ICs.

Limitations vs KO-1 Full

The KO-1 Lite is a capable entry point, but it makes deliberate tradeoffs to hit the $4,999 price point. Here is how it compares to the full KO-1 (Phase 3):

The primary limitations of the KO-1 Lite are positioning accuracy (lead screws vs ball screws) and particle control (no enclosure). Both directly limit yield at higher transistor counts. A 500-transistor circuit on the KO-1 Lite will have reasonable yield; a 2,000-transistor circuit will not, because stray particles on an open bench create defects that scale with die area and layer count.

The KO-1 Lite is designed for users who want to learn the process, build simple circuits, and decide whether they want to invest in the full KO-1. Early adopters who purchase the KO-1 Lite will receive a trade-in credit toward the KO-1 when it ships.
